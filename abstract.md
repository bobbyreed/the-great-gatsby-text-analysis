---

*The Great Gatsby* is routinely praised for its imagery, but that praise tends to
treat Fitzgerald's figures as ornament. This essay argues the opposite: that the
novel's three central claims — that the American dream is a beautiful fraud, that
wealth insulates the careless from consequence, and that the past cannot be
repeated and yet cannot be escaped — are advanced almost entirely through
figurative language. Metaphor in this novel is not the wrapping but the cargo; it
is the medium in which the book conducts its thinking. Reading closely and citing
by chapter and line, the essay organizes its evidence by claim, and within each
claim follows the relevant metaphors in the order they appear, so that each
argument can be seen to accumulate from the opening pages to the last. It gives
particular attention to figures that serve more than one claim — above all the
green light, which is at once the dream that dies upon attainment and the future
that forever recedes — and it closes on the novel's final sentence, "So we beat on,
boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past," as the single
image in which all three claims converge.
