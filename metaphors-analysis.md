# The Figurative Machinery of *The Great Gatsby*: A Study of Fitzgerald's Metaphors

*An analytical essay. Citations refer to the split chapter files in
`plain-text/chapterN/chapterN.md` and are given as (ch. N, l. NN). Quotations are
drawn from the public-domain text of F. Scott Fitzgerald's* The Great Gatsby
*(1925).*

---

## Introduction: metaphor as argument

It is a commonplace that *The Great Gatsby* is a beautifully written book, and the
praise usually settles on Fitzgerald's images — the green light, the eyes on the
billboard, the shirts thrown one by one onto the table. But to treat these as
decoration is to miss how the novel actually thinks. Fitzgerald does not state his
themes and then illustrate them; he argues them through metaphor. The book's
central claims — that the American dream is a beautiful fraud, that wealth
insulates the careless from consequence, that the past cannot be repeated and yet
cannot be escaped — are carried almost entirely in figurative language. The
metaphors are not the wrapping. They are the cargo.

This essay traces that figurative machinery through several clusters: the
metaphors of aspiration that culminate in the green light; the metaphors of decay
and judgment that gather in the valley of ashes and the eyes of Doctor T. J.
Eckleburg; the conversion of Daisy into a figure of money and music; Gatsby's
metaphysical self-invention; Fitzgerald's pervasive use of light, color, and
synesthesia; and finally the metaphors of water, time, and carelessness that close
the book. The argument throughout is that these are not isolated flourishes but a
single, coherent system. Nick Carraway, our narrator, is a man who reaches for a
metaphor whenever feeling exceeds his capacity to state it plainly, and the novel's
emotional logic lives in those reaches.

A note on method. Fitzgerald is as fond of the simile as of the metaphor proper,
and the two devices do different work. Where the distinction matters to the
reading I mark it; where the figure is functionally metaphoric regardless of its
grammatical form ("like," "as," or a bare equation) I treat it as part of the same
system. The point is not to police grammar but to follow the thought.

## I. The metaphors of aspiration: hope, machines, and the green light

The novel announces its preoccupation with hope in its first pages, and it does so
in metaphor. Nick tells us that Gatsby possessed "an extraordinary gift for hope, a
romantic readiness such as I have never found in any other person" (ch. 1, l. 9).
"Gift" is the operative figure: hope is imagined not as an attitude one chooses but
as an endowment, something parcelled out at birth — the same conceit Nick has just
used of moral decency, "a sense of the fundamental decencies is parcelled out
unequally at birth" (ch. 1, l. 7). Character, in Nick's imagination, is an
inheritance distributed by an indifferent hand. This quietly sets up the novel's
determinism: Gatsby's capacity for wonder is a thing he was given, not a thing he
earned, and it will destroy him.

Strikingly, the first sustained image attached to Gatsby is mechanical. His
"heightened sensitivity to the promises of life" makes him "related to one of those
intricate machines that register earthquakes ten thousand miles away" (ch. 1, l.
9). Gatsby is a seismograph. The comparison is doing something subtle: it makes his
hope a form of *receptivity*, an instrument tuned to register tremors of promise
that other people cannot feel. But it also makes him a machine, and the novel will
return again and again to the image of Gatsby as something wound up and running
down — most poignantly when, after the reunion with Daisy, he is "running down like
an overwound clock" (ch. 5, l. 229), his anticipation spent. The seismograph and
the overwound clock are the same metaphor at two ends of the book: an exquisitely
sensitive mechanism, built for one purpose, exhausting itself.

All of this aspiration finds its object in the green light. Fitzgerald introduces
it without explanation at the close of Chapter 1: Gatsby stretches his arms toward
the water and Nick sees "a single green light, minute and far away" (ch. 1, l.
305). The light is literal — it burns at the end of Daisy's dock — but the novel
metaphorizes it almost immediately into the vehicle for desire as such. A breath
earlier, Fitzgerald has already told us what that reaching means: Gatsby has come
out "to determine what share was his of our local heavens" (ch. 1, l. 303). The
metaphor turns the night sky into divisible property, and it adds a facet that the
seismograph image leaves out. Gatsby's hope is not only receptive; it is
acquisitive — a wonder that wants to *own* its share of the firmament. That
proprietary instinct is what fastens, a sentence later, onto the green light, and
it is also the first hint that a dream of pure aspiration is entangled, from the
start, with the urge to possess. The crucial
passage comes in Chapter 5, when Gatsby has actually got Daisy back and the symbol
collapses under the weight of fulfillment: "Possibly it had occurred to him that
the colossal significance of that light had now vanished forever... Now it was
again a green light on a dock. His count of enchanted objects had diminished by
one" (ch. 5, l. 243). This is one of the most important sentences in the book about
how metaphor works. The light had "colossal significance" only as long as it was
unattained; possession converts it back into mere fact, "again a green light on a
dock." Fitzgerald is showing us that the dream requires distance — that desire is
structurally a relationship to what is out of reach. The phrase "enchanted objects"
generalizes the point: Gatsby lives among things he has charged with meaning, and
the meaning drains the moment the gap closes.

The green light returns in the novel's final movement, no longer Gatsby's private
beacon but a figure for the whole human relationship to the future: "Gatsby
believed in the green light, the orgastic future that year by year recedes before
us. It eluded us then, but that's no matter—tomorrow we will run faster, stretch
out our arms farther" (ch. 9, l. 328). The metaphor has been universalized. The
reaching arms that were Gatsby's alone in Chapter 1 become "ours"; the green light
becomes the receding future itself. And the receding is the point — a future that
"year by year recedes before us" can never be reached by definition, which is why
the book ends not in arrival but in the famous image of futile motion: "So we beat
on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past" (ch. 9, l.
330). I will return to this sentence at the end, because it gathers the whole
system into a single figure.

## II. The valley of ashes and the eyes: metaphors of decay and judgment

If the green light is the novel's metaphor of aspiration, the valley of ashes is
its metaphor of consequence — the wasteland on which the careless dreams of the
rich are built. Fitzgerald constructs it as an extended, almost allegorical
conceit. It is "a fantastic farm where ashes grow like wheat into ridges and hills
and grotesque gardens" (ch. 2, l. 3). The agricultural metaphor is deliberate and
bitter: where a farm produces food and life, this one produces only ash, the
residue of combustion. The figure then extends to the people who live there, who
are themselves made of ash: the valley breeds "ash-grey men" who "swarm up with
leaden spades," and the ashes "take the forms of houses and chimneys and rising
smoke and finally, with a transcendent effort, of men who move dimly and already
crumbling through the powdery air" (ch. 2, l. 3). The laborers are not merely
covered in ash; metaphorically they *are* ash, half-formed and disintegrating even
as they move. This is the human cost of East and West Egg rendered as a single
sustained image, and it is no accident that George Wilson, the valley's
representative man, is repeatedly described in terms that dissolve him into his
surroundings — "mingling immediately with the cement color of the walls" (ch. 2, l.
35), so insubstantial that Myrtle walks "through her husband as if he were a ghost"
(ch. 2, l. 31).

Presiding over this wasteland are the eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg, the faded
advertisement that Fitzgerald slowly transforms from billboard into deity. From the
first they are personified — they "brood on over the solemn dumping ground" (ch. 2,
l. 5) — and the verb "brood" gives them consciousness, even melancholy. The
metaphor is completed not by Nick but by the broken Wilson in Chapter 8, who stares
out at the billboard and confuses it with God: "God sees everything," he says (ch.
8, l. 209), and Michaelis has to remind him, "That's an advertisement." The
juxtaposition is the entire argument: in the world of the novel, the only thing
left brooding over the ash-heap of consumer capitalism is an advertisement, and a
grieving man mistakes it for divine judgment. Fitzgerald lets the metaphor make the
theological point that he never states outright — that the transcendent has been
replaced by the commercial, and that the replacement is watching.

The eyes "keep their vigil" again in Chapter 7 as the action turns toward Myrtle's
death (ch. 7, l. 325), so that the billboard frames the catastrophe at both ends.
The valley of ashes is thus not a setting Fitzgerald visits once; it is a metaphor
he plants in Chapter 2 and detonates in Chapters 7 and 8, where its ash-figures and
brooding eyes collect the consequences of the careless lives playing out across the
bay.

And the valley does not merely collect the consequences; it sends them. When
Wilson finally crosses out of his wasteland to kill Gatsby, Fitzgerald describes
him as "that ashen, fantastic figure gliding toward him through the amorphous
trees" (ch. 8, l. 221). The metaphor turns the valley's representative man into a
piece of the ash itself — the wasteland made animate and vengeful, drifting into
the green, watered precincts of the dream. This is the nuance that completes the
section's argument: the figure of decay is not a static backdrop against which the
figure of aspiration shines. It is mobile. It walks across the bay and extinguishes
the dreamer in his own pool. The novel's two governing metaphors of geography —
the green world of aspiration and the grey world of consequence — finally meet on
Gatsby's lawn, and the ash wins.

## III. Daisy: the girl made of money and music

Daisy Buchanan is the novel's great example of a person turned, by metaphor, into
an idea. Nick repeatedly reaches for figures of music to capture her voice — "the
kind of voice that the ear follows up and down as if each speech is an arrangement
of notes that will never be played again" (ch. 1, l. 67) — and the musical metaphor
recurs whenever she speaks, her words a "singing compulsion" (ch. 1, l. 67), her
song carrying "a little of her warm human magic upon the air" (ch. 6, l. 221). But
the decisive metaphor for Daisy is Gatsby's, and it fuses the musical with the
financial. In Chapter 7, when Nick fumbles to describe her voice, Gatsby supplies
the answer: "Her voice is full of money" (ch. 7, l. 211). Nick immediately
recognizes its rightness and elaborates: "It was full of money—that was the
inexhaustible charm that rose and fell in it, the jingle of it, the cymbals' song
of it... High in a white palace the king's daughter, the golden girl" (ch. 7, l.
213).

This is perhaps the most analyzed sentence in the book, and rightly so, because it
performs a small miracle of compression. The "jingle" is the literal sound of coins
and the figurative music of her voice at once; "cymbals' song" makes wealth
audible; and "the king's daughter, the golden girl" lifts Daisy out of the realm of
persons entirely into fairy tale. What Gatsby loves, the metaphor confesses, is not
a woman but a sound — and the sound is money. The novel has been preparing this
recognition: in Chapter 8 we learn that Gatsby was overwhelmed by "the youth and
mystery that wealth imprisons and preserves" (ch. 8, l. 29) and that Daisy seemed to
gleam "like silver, safe and proud above the hot struggles of the poor" (ch. 8, l.
29). She is precious metal — silver, then gold — and the metaphor quietly indicts
the dream it describes. Gatsby's love is inseparable from the wealth that makes
Daisy shine, which is why the green light burned at the end of *her* dock and why
her voice, in the end, sounds like coins.

The metaphors of music complicate the metaphors of money in one telling way.
In Chapter 5 Nick says that her voice "couldn't be over-dreamed—that voice was a
deathless song" (ch. 5, l. 310). This is the single quality the novel grants Daisy
without irony: not her face, not her constancy, but the sound she makes. And the
reason the voice can be "deathless" where the rest of her is perishable is that a
voice is pure abstraction — music that will hold whatever meaning is poured into
it. The nuance matters. Everything else about Daisy eventually "tumbled short of
his dreams," but her voice did not, precisely because it was never substance to
begin with. The metaphor of money and the metaphor of immortal song thus name the
same fatal quality from two directions: an enchantment that is entirely real as
sound and entirely empty as feeling.

Even Daisy's own metaphors participate in this. When she calls Nick "an absolute
rose" (ch. 1, l. 185), the figure is charming and meaningless — Nick notes flatly,
"I am not even faintly like a rose" — and its emptiness is characterizing. Daisy
deals in pretty figures that do not refer to anything, just as her voice promises a
warmth it cannot deliver. The metaphor of the voice that is "full of money" finally
explains the hollowness: her enchantment is real, but what it is full of is not
feeling.

## IV. Gatsby's self-invention: Platonic conceptions and the incarnation

The most metaphysically ambitious metaphors in the book are reserved for Gatsby's
self-creation, and they are deliberately religious. Fitzgerald tells us in Chapter 6
that "Jay Gatsby... sprang from his Platonic conception of himself" (ch. 6, l. 15) —
that is, the man invented an ideal Form of himself and then strove to embody it.
The figure is philosophical, but the sentence that follows is scriptural: "He was a
son of God—a phrase which, if it means anything, means just that—and he must be
about His Father's Business, the service of a vast, vulgar and meretricious beauty"
(ch. 6, l. 15). The blasphemy is precise and intentional. Gatsby is cast as a Christ
who serves not God but "meretricious beauty," the false glamour of wealth; his
"Father's Business" is the pursuit of money and Daisy. By borrowing the language of
incarnation, Fitzgerald elevates Gatsby's delusion to the level of a religion and,
in the same breath, exposes what that religion worships.

If the religion has a god in "meretricious beauty," it also has a quest.
Fitzgerald completes the figure in Chapter 8: Gatsby "had committed himself to the
following of a grail" (ch. 8, l. 27). The grail metaphor supplies the chivalric
dimension that the static "Platonic conception" lacks — it converts self-invention
into a *vocation*, a sacred errand demanding fidelity to the end. This adds a
crucial nuance to the section's argument and reaches back to the green light:
a grail is not enjoyed, it is sought, and the knight is defined entirely by the
seeking. It explains why Gatsby cannot simply win Daisy and be content, and why the
brief possession of Chapter 5 begins, almost at once, to unmake him. To attain the
grail is to end the quest that constitutes the self; for a man who "sprang from his
Platonic conception of himself," arrival is a kind of death.

The incarnation metaphor pays off in the novel's account of the original kiss. As
Gatsby kisses Daisy for the first time, "he forever wed his unutterable visions to
her perishable breath" (ch. 6, l. 269), and "at his lips' touch she blossomed for
him like a flower and the incarnation was complete" (ch. 6, l. 269). The vocabulary
is theological — "visions," "incarnation" — and the tragedy is built into the
metaphor: he has wedded eternal "visions" to "perishable breath," the infinite to
the mortal. Daisy the woman can never sustain the weight of the idea Gatsby has
made her carry, which is exactly what Nick concludes in Chapter 5: "There must have
been moments even that afternoon when Daisy tumbled short of his dreams—not through
her own fault but because of the colossal vitality of his illusion" (ch. 5, l.
308). "The colossal vitality of his illusion" personifies the dream as something
more alive than the woman who inspired it. Gatsby has "thrown himself into it with a
creative passion, adding to it all the time, decking it out with every bright
feather that drifted his way" (ch. 5, l. 308) — the dream is a thing he builds and
feathers like a nest, and "no amount of fire or freshness can challenge what a man
will store up in his ghostly heart" (ch. 5, l. 308). The heart is "ghostly," a
haunted storehouse; the illusion outlives and outweighs reality.

Notice how consistently these metaphors point the same direction. Gatsby is a
Platonic Form, a son of God, an incarnation, a man with a ghostly heart — every
figure pushes him toward the abstract and the eternal, and every figure thereby
sets up his collision with the perishable, the literal, the merely real. When the
illusion finally shatters in Chapter 8, Fitzgerald reaches for one more metaphor of
fragile construction: "'Jay Gatsby' had broken up like glass against Tom's hard
malice" (ch. 8, l. 19). The invented self — the name in quotation marks — was
glass: beautiful, transparent, and brittle.

## V. Light, color, and synesthesia

Across the novel Fitzgerald runs a current of metaphors that treat light and color
as substances, and that cross the senses freely. This synesthetic habit is most
visible in the party chapters. The orchestra plays "yellow cocktail music" (ch. 3,
l. 11), a phrase that paints sound; the talk of the crowd becomes "the opera of
voices" that "pitches a key higher" (ch. 3, l. 11); laughter is poured like liquid,
"spilled with prodigality, tipped out at a cheerful word" (ch. 3, l. 11). At
Gatsby's, the very flowers are described in cross-sensory terms — "the sparkling
odor of jonquils and the frothy odor of hawthorn and plum blossoms and the pale
gold odor of kiss-me-at-the-gate" (ch. 5, l. 217) — where smell is given sparkle,
froth, and color in turn. The effect is not mere prettiness; it conveys a world in
which sensation has become unmoored and intoxicating, where everything is
convertible into everything else, much as money in this society converts into
beauty, status, and love.

Fitzgerald pushes that convertibility to its sharpest point when Nick sees
Manhattan from the Queensboro Bridge as "the city rising up across the river in
white heaps and sugar lumps all built with a wish out of non-olfactory money"
(ch. 4, l. 111). "Sugar lumps" makes the skyline edible; "non-olfactory money" is
the stranger figure, money defined by the smell it does *not* have. The phrase
quietly launders wealth through the senses — scrubbing away the odor that money
might otherwise carry, of labor, of the valley of ashes, of corruption — so that
the city can appear as pure confection "built with a wish." The nuance this adds is
that synesthesia in the novel is not only intoxication but a kind of moral
deodorizing: the same sensory alchemy that turns sound yellow and odor gold can
also turn dirty money into sugar. Fitzgerald's cross-sensory metaphors are
beautiful, and they are quietly complicit.

Light, in particular, behaves like a fluid or a fire. Gatsby's house "was blazing
with light" on the night he cannot sleep (ch. 5, l. 3); when Daisy enters his life
again "he literally glowed; without a word or a gesture of exultation a new
well-being radiated from him and filled the little room" (ch. 5, l. 175). Joy is
luminous, an emanation. Even sunlight rings: when the rain stops, there are
"twinkle-bells of sunshine in the room" (ch. 5, l. 181), and Gatsby smiles "like an
ecstatic patron of recurrent light" (ch. 5, l. 181). The recurring association of
Gatsby with light — he dispenses "starlight to casual moths" (ch. 4, l. 312) — sets
up the cruelty of his death scene, where the light goes out of the metaphors
altogether and the world becomes "material without being real, where poor ghosts,
breathing dreams like air, drifted fortuitously about" (ch. 8, l. 221). Once the
dream is dead, Fitzgerald drains the color and luminosity from the prose, and the
reader feels the loss as a literal dimming.

The moth, worth pausing on, is one of the book's quietest and most damning
recurring figures. Gatsby's guests "came and went like moths among the whisperings
and the champagne and the stars" (ch. 3, l. 3), and later he is said to have
"dispensed starlight to casual moths" (ch. 4, l. 312). Moths are drawn to light
they cannot reach and that may destroy them; calling the partygoers moths makes
their glittering pleasure faintly doomed, and it places Gatsby himself in the role
of the flame. But Gatsby is also, in his own way, a moth — drawn across the bay
toward the green light. The metaphor implicates everyone.

## VI. Water, current, and the metaphors of time

The novel begins and ends on water, and Fitzgerald uses the sea, the Sound, and the
current as his master metaphors for time and the irrecoverable past. Early on the
imagery is comic and domestic — Long Island Sound is "the great wet barnyard" (ch.
1, l. 27) — but as the book proceeds water becomes the medium of loss. Gatsby, on
the train leaving Louisville after the war, "stretched out his hand desperately as
if to snatch only a wisp of air, to save a fragment of the spot that she had made
lovely for him" (ch. 8, l. 61); the past is already as ungraspable as air. By the
final page the metaphor has hardened into the image that governs the whole book.

That final passage deserves close reading because it is the novel's metaphors
brought to a point. Lying on the beach, Nick imagines the vanished continent the
Dutch sailors first saw: "a fresh, green breast of the new world" whose trees "had
once pandered in whispers to the last and greatest of all human dreams" (ch. 9, l.
324). The land is figured as a woman's body — nurturing, seductive — and as a pander,
a procurer who whispers to human desire. The green of that "green breast" rhymes
with the green light, fusing Gatsby's private longing with the continent's founding
promise: both are green, both are objects of insatiable wonder, both recede. Nick
then makes the link explicit, observing that Gatsby's dream "was already behind him,
somewhere back in that vast obscurity beyond the city, where the dark fields of the
republic rolled on under the night" (ch. 9, l. 326). The nation itself becomes a
dark rolling field, and the dream is located in the past, "behind him," even as
Gatsby reaches forward.

Water in the novel is not merely the medium of loss; it is morally indifferent to
it, and that indifference is its own facet of the metaphor. Preparing to leave the
East, Nick says he wanted "to leave things in order and not just trust that
obliging and indifferent sea to sweep my refuse away" (ch. 9, l. 278). The sea is
"obliging" — it will take whatever you give it — but "indifferent," and the pairing
says something the current image alone does not: time and water neither clean up
after us nor hold us to account. That is exactly why Nick feels he must act, and
exactly what Tom and Daisy rely on when they retreat into their money. The same
sea that bears the boats "back ceaselessly into the past" will also, if allowed,
sweep the refuse away and remember nothing. The current that defeats aspiration and
the tide that absolves carelessness are the same indifferent water.

And so the book closes on the current: "Gatsby believed in the green light, the
orgastic future that year by year recedes before us... So we beat on, boats against
the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past" (ch. 9, l. 328–330). The
metaphor is perfectly self-defeating, which is its genius. We row forward ("beat
on") toward a future ("the green light") that by definition recedes, while the
current of time carries us backward into the past. Effort and outcome point in
opposite directions. Every metaphor of aspiration in the book — the reaching arms,
the seismograph tuned to promise, the moth at the flame — is here resolved into a
single image of strenuous, beautiful, doomed motion. The boats are us; the current
is time; and the destination is, paradoxically, the past we are trying to escape.

## VII. The body, the machine, and the metaphor of carelessness

A final cluster deserves attention because it carries the novel's moral verdict.
Fitzgerald characterizes Tom and Daisy Buchanan through metaphors of force,
machinery, and physical mass, and he indicts them through a metaphor of motion. Tom
is introduced as a body of weaponized power: "Two shining, arrogant eyes had
established dominance over his face" (ch. 1, l. 39), and his is "a body capable of
enormous leverage—a cruel body" (ch. 1, l. 39). "Leverage" is a mechanical term;
Tom is an engine of force, and the metaphor anticipates the casual violence with
which he breaks Myrtle's nose and, later, sends Wilson toward Gatsby. When his
control over his wife and mistress begins to slip in Chapter 7, panic is rendered
as a lash: "Tom was feeling the hot whips of panic," his women "slipping
precipitately from his control" (ch. 7, l. 329).

The Buchanan habit of handling people as objects is established long before any
violence, in a metaphor small enough to miss. At the first dinner Tom, "wedging
his tense arm imperatively under mine," compels Nick out of the room "as though he
were moving a checker to another square" (ch. 1, l. 125). A checker is not
persuaded or invited; it is repositioned. The figure names, in miniature, the trait
the famous verdict will later enlarge — that before the Buchanans smash things and
creatures, they move people like pieces in a game. This is the nuance the driving
metaphor needs: carelessness does not begin as malice but as *play*, an idle
confidence that other lives exist to be slid about the board at will. The literal
wreckage on the Valley road is only that game played at fifty miles an hour.

But the governing metaphor for the Buchanans is driving — careless driving — and
Fitzgerald builds it patiently. In Chapter 3 Jordan Baker, herself "incurably
dishonest," supplies the principle in a conversation about cars: a bad driver is
safe "until she met another bad driver" (recalled at ch. 9, l. 290). The car, the
period's great emblem of freedom and wealth, becomes the novel's emblem of
heedless privilege, and the metaphor is paid off in literal blood when Daisy runs
down Myrtle and lets Gatsby take the blame. Nick's final judgment converts the
literal carelessness into a moral one in a sentence that turns "careless" into the
book's harshest figure: "They were careless people, Tom and Daisy—they smashed up
things and creatures and then retreated back into their money or their vast
carelessness... and let other people clean up the mess they had made" (ch. 9, l.
314). "Retreated back into their money" treats wealth as a fortress, a place one can
withdraw into for protection from consequence — the exact inverse of the valley of
ashes, where the poor have no such refuge. The metaphor of the careless driver and
the metaphor of money-as-fortress are the same indictment: the rich do damage and
drive on.

## Conclusion: a single system

What emerges from this survey is that Fitzgerald's metaphors are not scattered
ornaments but a single, tightly integrated system, and that the system *is* the
novel's argument. The green light, the reaching arms, and the boats against the
current form one continuous figure for hope and its impossibility. The valley of
ashes and the brooding eyes form a second figure for the human and moral cost of
that hope. Daisy's voice "full of money" and her gleam "like silver" reveal what the
dream actually worships. Gatsby's Platonic conception of himself, his ghostly heart,
and the glass into which his invented self finally shatters trace the rise and
destruction of a man who tried to live inside an idea. And the metaphors of light
and color, switched on for the parties and switched off at his death, let the reader
feel the dream's vitality and its extinction at the level of the sentence.

The deepest of these figures is the last one. "So we beat on, boats against the
current, borne back ceaselessly into the past" (ch. 9, l. 330) is the novel's whole
metaphorical machinery compressed into eleven words. It contains the forward-reaching
hope (we beat on), the resisting medium of time (the current), and the tragic
outcome (borne back into the past). Fitzgerald could have stated his theme — that
Americans pursue a future they imagine as new but that is really a lost past
they can never return to — as a proposition. Instead he gave us a picture of boats
on a river, and trusted the metaphor to carry the thought farther than any
statement could. That trust is the source of the book's enduring power. In *The
Great Gatsby*, the metaphors do not illustrate the meaning. They are the meaning.
