## 2. Outline

A two-part thesis runs through the whole: **(content)** the novel makes three
claims about American life; **(method)** it makes them almost entirely through
metaphor. The body is organized **by claim**, and within each claim the evidence is
walked in **book order**, gathered into thematic sub-arcs that each move forward
through the chapters.

### Introduction: three claims, one method
- States the two-part thesis: the three claims + "the metaphors are not the
  wrapping, they are the cargo."
- Names the structural problem: the figures form a **web, not a grid** — single
  images serve multiple claims.
- States the organizing choice: impose a grid (by claim), sub-ordered by book
  order; give each cross-claim figure a primary home and flag it elsewhere.
- Flags the green light as deliberately appearing in **I** and **III**.

### I. The American dream is a beautiful fraud
*Carried by metaphors of aspiration and enchantment — the dream and its object are
luminous, valuable, and finally hollow.*
- **Sub-arc A — the dream / green light:** "gift for hope" as an endowment (ch. 1) →
  "what share was his of our local heavens," wonder and possession as one gesture
  (ch. 1) → the green light introduced (ch. 1) → it collapses on attainment, "his
  count of enchanted objects had diminished by one" (ch. 5). *Point: the dream
  requires distance; it cannot survive being attained.*
- **Sub-arc B — Daisy:** the voice as "notes that will never be played again"
  (ch. 1) → "Her voice is full of money" / "the golden girl" (ch. 7) → gleaming
  "like silver" above the poor (ch. 8) → "a deathless song," transcendent because
  pure abstraction (ch. 5). *Point: what the dream worships is enchantment real as
  sound, empty as feeling.*
- **Sub-arc C — the invented self:** "Platonic conception of himself," "son of
  God," "meretricious beauty" (ch. 6) → the "incarnation," wedding "unutterable
  visions to her perishable breath" (ch. 6) → "'Jay Gatsby' had broken up like
  glass" (ch. 8). *Point: the self is a beautiful, brittle fabrication.*

### II. Wealth insulates the careless from consequence
*Carried by metaphors of force and machinery, the ash-figures of the valley, and
money as a fortress. Through-line: the careless do damage and drive on; the cost
falls on those with no money to retreat into.*
- **Sub-arc A — the Buchanans as force:** carelessness as play, moving Nick "like a
  checker" (ch. 1); Tom's "cruel body" capable of "enormous leverage" (ch. 1); "the
  hot whips of panic" when control slips (ch. 7); the careless-driver principle
  (ch. 9). *Point: they handle people as objects.*
- **Sub-arc B — who pays (the valley):** ashes "grow like wheat," ash-men "already
  crumbling" (ch. 2); Wilson "mingling with the cement color of the walls," Myrtle
  walking "through her husband as if he were a ghost" (ch. 2); Eckleburg the
  advertisement mistaken for God, "God sees everything" (ch. 8); Wilson as "that
  ashen, fantastic figure gliding" who kills the dreamer, not the careless (ch. 8).
  *Point: consequence falls on the un-insulated.*
- **Sub-arc C — the insulation named:** "they retreated back into their money," wealth
  as a fortress (ch. 9); the city "built with a wish out of non-olfactory money,"
  money that has been deodorized (ch. 4). *Point: money is the medium of
  insulation.*

### III. The past cannot be repeated and yet cannot be escaped
*Carried by metaphors of time and water; contains the book's central
contradiction.*
- The creed: "Can't repeat the past?... Why of course you can!" (ch. 6).
- The unrepeatable moment: the sidewalk "ladder" to "the incomparable milk of
  wonder" (ch. 6); after the failed reunion, "running down like an overwound clock"
  (ch. 5). *Point: the past cannot be wound back up.*
- Water as irreversibility: reaching "to snatch only a wisp of air" leaving
  Louisville (ch. 8); the "obliging and indifferent sea" that remembers nothing
  (ch. 9). *Point: the medium of loss is also morally indifferent.*
- The culmination: the "fresh, green breast of the new world" rhyming with the green
  light (ch. 9); "the dark fields of the republic" (ch. 9); the green light as "the
  orgastic future that year by year recedes" (ch. 9); closing on "boats against the
  current, borne back ceaselessly into the past" (ch. 9).

### Conclusion: one figure, three claims
- The final sentence holds all three claims at once: "we beat on" (the dream),
  "boats against the current" (the careless borne by indifferent water), "borne back
  ceaselessly into the past" (irreversible time).
- The green light's doubleness restated: the dream that dies on attainment (I) and
  the future that recedes (III).
- Returns to the method thesis: in this novel the metaphors do not illustrate the
  meaning — they are the meaning.

## Structural notes (for ongoing revision)

- **Book order is approximate, not strict.** Each claim is built from thematic
  sub-arcs that are individually near-chronological, but the section steps back in
  chapter number between sub-arcs (e.g., Section I runs ch. 1 → 5, then ch. 1 → 7 →
  8 → 5, then ch. 6 → 8). This is the honest residue of the "web vs. grid" problem.
  If we want stricter linearity, the fix is to interleave the sub-arcs by chapter —
  at some cost to each sub-arc's internal momentum.
- **Claim II is the heaviest, Claim III the leanest in sources** but tightest in
  payoff (it owns the ending). Worth watching as we grow the essay toward ten pages
  that we don't over-weight II.
- **Cross-claim figures to keep flagged:** the green light (I + III), non-olfactory
  money (I + II), the indifferent sea (II + III).
