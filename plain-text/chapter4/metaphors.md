# Metaphors — Chapter 4

A scholarly catalogue of the metaphors in Chapter 4 of *The Great Gatsby*. Each
entry quotes the figurative language and notes what is being compared (tenor →
vehicle). Genuine metaphors are listed first; notable similes follow.

## Metaphors

- **"the world and its mistress returned to Gatsby's house and twinkled hilariously on his lawn"** — society personified as "the world and its mistress."
- **"the subtle tribute of knowing nothing whatever about him"** — willful ignorance reframed as a form of homage.
- **"the grey names"** of the old timetable — names drained of color/life with the decaying paper.
- **"His right hand suddenly ordered divine retribution to stand by."** — Gatsby's gesture as a command summoning heaven.
- **"the memory of that sudden extinction of a clan still haunted him"** — family death as extinction.
- **"the very phrases were worn so threadbare"** — clichés as worn-out cloth.
- **"My incredulity was submerged in fascination now"** — disbelief drowned.
- **"With fenders spread like wings we scattered light through half Astoria"** — the car as a winged creature.
- **"the city rising up across the river in white heaps and sugar lumps all built with a wish out of non-olfactory money"** — Manhattan as sugar/confection conjured from clean money.
- **"The city seen from the Queensboro Bridge is always the city seen for the first time, in its first wild promise of all the mystery and the beauty in the world."** — the skyline as embodied promise.
- **"the single-mindedness of a burglar blowing a safe"** — Wolfsheim's corruption of the World's Series figured as a safe-cracking job.
- **"one man could start to play with the faith of fifty million people"** — public faith as a plaything.
- **"a turbaned 'character' leaking sawdust at every pore"** — Gatsby's tall tales as a stuffed, sawdust-leaking dummy.
- **"He came alive to me, delivered suddenly from the womb of his purposeless splendor."** — Gatsby's emergence as a birth.
- **"he dispensed starlight to casual moths"** — Gatsby's parties as starlight, guests again as moths.
- **"his whole statement fell to pieces"** — the lie as a shattering object.
- **"the red, white and blue banners ... stretched out stiff and said tut-tut-tut-tut in a disapproving way"** — the flags personified as scolding chaperones.
- **"the holocaust"** / and earlier his **"broken heart"** eased by **"crimson-lighted depths"** of rubies — grief figured through gemstone light.
- **"There are only the pursued, the pursuing, the busy and the tired."** — all human life sorted into four pursuing/exhausted types.
- **"no girl whose disembodied face floated along the dark cornices and blinding signs"** — desire as a face haunting the cityscape.

## Notable similes and related figurative language

- "swollen here and there in its monstrous length with triumphant hatboxes ... terraced with a labyrinth of windshields that mirrored a dozen suns" — Gatsby's car as a monstrous, sun-mirroring edifice.
- "With fenders spread **like** wings" — the car as winged.
- "in white heaps and sugar lumps" — the city as sugar.
- "the skins of tigers flaming in his palace on the Grand Canal" — Nick's credulous vision of Gatsby's fabricated past.
- "as drunk as a monkey" — Daisy on her wedding eve.
- "lovely **as** the June night in her flowered dress" — Daisy.
- "coming to pieces **like** snow" — the wet letter dissolving.
- "the clear voices of girls, already gathered **like** crickets on the grass" — girls as crickets.
- "He hurried the phrase ... or swallowed it or choked on it" — Gatsby's lie figured as food.
