# Metaphors — Chapter 9

A scholarly catalogue of the metaphors in Chapter 9 of *The Great Gatsby* — the
funeral and Nick's closing meditation, home to the novel's most famous figures.
Each entry quotes the figurative language and notes what is being compared
(tenor → vehicle). Genuine metaphors are listed first; notable similes follow.

## Metaphors

- **"an endless drill of police and photographers and newspaper men"** — the aftermath as a military drill.
- **"the adventitious authority of his voice set the key for the newspaper reports"** — tone of voice as a musical key the press tunes to.
- **"every surmise about him ... was referred to me"** — Nick made the clearinghouse of Gatsby's memory.
- **"a feeling of defiance, of scornful solidarity between Gatsby and me against them all"** — alliance with the dead.
- **"his protest continued in my brain"** — Gatsby's imagined voice persisting.
- **"a token of forgotten violence staring down from the wall"** — Cody's photograph as a relic of violence.
- **"his eyes leaking isolated and unpunctual tears"** — grief figured mechanically as leaking.
- **"the long green tickets clasped tight in our gloved hands"** — within the Midwest reverie, the tickets as talismans of belonging.
- **"that vast obscurity beyond the city, where the dark fields of the republic rolled on under the night"** — the nation as dark rolling fields.
- **"a fresh, green breast of the new world"** — the primal American continent as a nurturing breast.
- **"Its vanished trees ... had once pandered in whispers to the last and greatest of all human dreams"** — the land as a pander/seducer to human aspiration.
- **"face to face for the last time in history with something commensurate to his capacity for wonder"** — the continent as the last adequate object of human wonder.
- **"the orgastic future that year by year recedes before us"** — the future as a receding, climactic horizon.
- **"Gatsby believed in the green light"** — the green light as the supreme symbol of hope/desire (the novel's closing emblem).
- **"It eluded us then, but that's no matter—tomorrow we will run faster, stretch out our arms farther"** — aspiration as a footrace/reaching.
- **"So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past."** — humanity as boats rowing against a current that bears them backward (the novel's final metaphor for futile striving against time).
- **"They were careless people, Tom and Daisy—they smashed up things and creatures and then retreated back into their money or their vast carelessness"** — money/carelessness as a fortress to retreat into.
- **"this huge incoherent failure of a house"** — Gatsby's mansion as an emblem of his failed dream.
- **"that obliging and indifferent sea to sweep my refuse away"** — moral residue figured as refuse for the sea.

## Notable similes and related figurative language

- "served up in racy pasquinade" — the scandal as a dish to be served.
- "I see it as a night scene by El Greco" — West Egg likened to an El Greco painting (the extended grotesque vision of the men carrying the drunken woman).
- "her hair the color of an autumn leaf" — Jordan.
- "looking cheerful **as** Christmas itself" — the Midwest railroad cars.
- "we melted indistinguishably into it again" — the returning Westerners merging with their region.
- "I felt suddenly as though I were talking to a child" — Tom.
- "the inessential houses began to melt away" — the shore at moonrise dissolving to reveal the primal island.
- "his dream must have seemed so close that he could hardly fail to grasp it" — the dream as a graspable object.
