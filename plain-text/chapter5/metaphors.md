# Metaphors — Chapter 5

A scholarly catalogue of the metaphors in Chapter 5 of *The Great Gatsby* — the
novel's pivot, Gatsby's reunion with Daisy. Each entry quotes the figurative
language and notes what is being compared (tenor → vehicle). Genuine metaphors
are listed first; notable similes follow.

## Metaphors

- **"the whole corner of the peninsula was blazing with light"** — Gatsby's over-lit house as a blaze/fire.
- **"as if the house had winked into the darkness"** — the house personified, winking.
- **"The exhilarating ripple of her voice was a wild tonic in the rain."** — Daisy's voice as a medicinal tonic.
- **"A damp streak of hair lay like a dash of blue paint across her cheek"** — hair as a brushstroke of paint.
- **"his hands plunged like weights in his coat pockets"** — hands as dead weights.
- **"He literally glowed; ... a new well-being radiated from him and filled the little room."** — joy as light/radiance emanating from Gatsby.
- **"there were twinkle-bells of sunshine in the room"** — sunlight as ringing bells (synesthesia).
- **"he smiled like a weather man, like an ecstatic patron of recurrent light"** — Gatsby as a worshipper/patron of light.
- **"Her throat, full of aching, grieving beauty, told only of her unexpected joy."** — emotion lodged bodily in the voice.
- **"his possessions ... none of it was any longer real"** — reality itself contingent on Daisy's gaze.
- **"running down like an overwound clock"** — Gatsby's spent anticipation as a wound-down mechanism.
- **"shirts ... piled like bricks in stacks a dozen high"** — shirts as bricks (the dream's material accumulation).
- **"the colossal significance of that light had now vanished forever"** — the green light as a vessel of "colossal significance," now emptied.
- **"It had seemed as close as a star to the moon. Now it was again a green light on a dock. His count of enchanted objects had diminished by one."** — the demythologizing of the symbol; objects as "enchanted."
- **"the colossal vitality of his illusion"** — the dream personified as a living, vital force.
- **"He had thrown himself into it with a creative passion, adding to it all the time, decking it out with every bright feather that drifted his way."** — the illusion built/feathered like a nest or costume.
- **"No amount of fire or freshness can challenge what a man will store up in his ghostly heart."** — the heart as a haunted storehouse.
- **"that voice was a deathless song"** — Daisy's voice as immortal music.
- **"possessed by intense life"** — the lovers as ones seized/possessed.
- **"the sparkling odor of jonquils and the frothy odor of hawthorn ... and the pale gold odor of kiss-me-at-the-gate"** — synesthetic metaphors giving scent sparkle, froth, and color.

## Notable similes and related figurative language

- "Your place looks **like** the world's fair" — the lit house as a world's fair.
- "occasional thin drops swam **like** dew" — rain as dew.
- "pale **as** death" — Gatsby at the door.
- "turned sharply **as if** he were on a wire" — Gatsby's jerky movement.
- "like Kant at his church steeple" — Nick staring at Gatsby's house compared to Kant contemplating his steeple.
- "abounded in small muddy swamps and prehistoric marshes" — the rain-soaked lawn hyperbolically aged.
- "a huge black knotted tree whose massed leaves made a fabric against the rain" — leaves as woven cloth.
- "I'd like to just get one of those pink clouds and put you in it and push you around" — Daisy's whimsical conceit of clouds as furniture.
- "the pale gold odor of kiss-me-at-the-gate" — flower scent given color.
