# Metaphors — Chapter 6

A scholarly catalogue of the metaphors in Chapter 6 of *The Great Gatsby* — the
chapter that reveals James Gatz's origins. Each entry quotes the figurative
language and notes what is being compared (tenor → vehicle). Genuine metaphors
are listed first; notable similes follow.

## Metaphors

- **"The truth was that Jay Gatsby, of West Egg, Long Island, sprang from his Platonic conception of himself."** — Gatsby as a self-authored idea/Platonic form.
- **"He was a son of God ... and he must be about His Father's Business, the service of a vast, vulgar and meretricious beauty."** — Gatsby's self-invention figured in scriptural terms; beauty as the deity he serves.
- **"his heart was in a constant, turbulent riot"** — the heart as a rioting crowd.
- **"A universe of ineffable gaudiness spun itself out in his brain"** — imagination as a self-spinning cosmos.
- **"the unreality of reality, a promise that the rock of the world was founded securely on a fairy's wing"** — the world's foundation imagined as resting on a fairy's wing.
- **"dismayed at its ferocious indifference to the drums of his destiny"** — destiny as a beating drum.
- **"played Madame de Maintenon to his weakness and sent him to sea"** — Ella Kaye cast, by historical analogy, as a manipulating mistress.
- **"the vague contour of Jay Gatsby had filled out to the substantiality of a man"** — the invented self as a sketch gaining solid form.
- **"his singularly appropriate education"** — Cody's tutelage ironically named an "education."
- **"a world complete in itself, with its own standards and its own great figures"** — West Egg as a self-contained universe.
- **"a pervading harshness ... an unpleasantness in the air"** — mood as atmosphere.
- **"Daisy's voice was playing murmurous tricks in her throat"** — the voice as a sly trickster.
- **"a gorgeous, scarcely human orchid of a woman who sat in state under a white plum tree"** — the actress as an orchid.
- **"each change tipped out a little of her warm human magic upon the air"** — Daisy's singing as poured magic.
- **"What had amused me then turned septic on the air now."** — stale charm figured as an infection.
- **"the too obtrusive fate that herded its inhabitants along a short cut from nothing to nothing"** — fate personified as a herdsman driving cattle.
- **"the blocks of the sidewalk really formed a ladder and mounted to a secret place above the trees"** — the pavement as a ladder to transcendence.
- **"he could ... suck on the pap of life, gulp down the incomparable milk of wonder"** — life/wonder as nourishing milk.
- **"the tuning fork that had been struck upon a star"** — the moment of romantic vision as a tuning fork struck on a star.
- **"his mind would never romp again like the mind of God"** — Gatsby's imaginative freedom equated, then forfeited, to divine play.
- **"forever wed his unutterable visions to her perishable breath"** — abstract vision married to mortal flesh.
- **"At his lips' touch she blossomed for him like a flower and the incarnation was complete."** — Daisy as a blossoming flower; the union as an "incarnation" (the dream made flesh).
- **"the dim incalculable hours"** — time figured as an immeasurable expanse.

## Notable similes and related figurative language

- "an orchid of a woman" — the movie star as orchid.
- "their faces were touching except for a pale thin ray of moonlight between" — the director and star almost merged.
- "my lips parted **like** a dumb man's" — Nick straining to recover a lost phrase.
- "an elusive rhythm, a fragment of lost words" — memory as half-heard music.
