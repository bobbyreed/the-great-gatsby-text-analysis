# Metaphors — Chapter 1

A scholarly catalogue of the metaphors in Chapter 1 of *The Great Gatsby*. Each
entry quotes the figurative language and notes what is being compared (tenor →
vehicle). Genuine metaphors are listed first; notable similes and other
figurative language follow, kept separate so the devices are not conflated.

## Metaphors

- **"a sense of the fundamental decencies is parcelled out unequally at birth"** — moral character imagined as a finite commodity rationed out.
- **"Conduct may be founded on the hard rock or the wet marshes"** — human behavior figured as a building resting on solid or unstable ground.
- **"I wanted the world to be in uniform and at a sort of moral attention forever"** — a desire for moral order rendered as a military formation.
- **"riotous excursions with privileged glimpses into the human heart"** — emotional intimacy as a trespasser's tour.
- **"what foul dust floated in the wake of his dreams"** — Gatsby's dreams as a ship leaving a polluted wake.
- **"the abortive sorrows and short-winded elations of men"** — emotions given the failing breath/body of living things.
- **"an extraordinary gift for hope, a romantic readiness"** — hope as an innate talent or endowment.
- **"the warm center of the world"** / **"the ragged edge of the universe"** — the Middle West reimagined spatially as cosmic center vs. frayed border.
- **"so much fine health to be pulled down out of the young breath-giving air"** — vitality treated as fruit to be harvested from the air.
- **"they stood on my shelf in red and gold ... promising to unfold the shining secrets that only Midas and Morgan and Maecenas knew"** — finance books as gilded oracles of wealth.
- **"the great wet barnyard of Long Island Sound"** — the Sound as a domesticated farmyard.
- **"the white palaces of fashionable East Egg glittered along the water"** — mansions as palaces.
- **"the frosted wedding cake of the ceiling"** — the ornate ceiling as iced confection.
- **"the caught wind died out about the room"** — wind given mortality.
- **"the two young women ballooned slowly to the floor"** — the women as deflating balloons (metaphor carried in the verb).
- **"an intimate revelation was quivering on the horizon"** — a confession figured as weather/a dawning event.
- **"It was a body capable of enormous leverage—a cruel body."** — Tom's physique as a machine/instrument of force.
- **"Two shining, arrogant eyes had established dominance over his face"** — the eyes as conquering agents ruling the territory of the face.
- **"each speech is an arrangement of notes that will never be played again"** — Daisy's voice as music/score.
- **"a singing compulsion, a whispered 'Listen,' a promise ..."** — her voice as enchantment.
- **"the crimson room bloomed with light"** — the lit room as a flower opening.
- **"her heart was trying to come out to you concealed in one of those breathless, thrilling words"** — speech as a vessel smuggling the heart.
- **"You remind me of a—of a rose, an absolute rose."** — Daisy's metaphor naming Nick a rose.
- **"this fifth guest's shrill metallic urgency"** — the telephone (Tom's mistress) personified as an unwelcome dinner guest.
- **"his sturdy physical egotism no longer nourished his peremptory heart"** — egotism as food for the heart.
- **"Something was making him nibble at the edge of stale ideas"** — ideas as stale food.
- **"the silver pepper of the stars"** — stars as scattered pepper/seasoning.
- **"come out to determine what share was his of our local heavens"** — the sky as divisible property.
- **"a single green light, minute and far away"** — the green light, the novel's governing symbol of longing, introduced here.
- **"I was a guide, a pathfinder, an original settler."** — Nick aggrandizes a trivial encounter into frontier myth.
- **"the unquiet darkness"** — night given restless emotion.

## Notable similes and related figurative language

- "as if he were related to one of those intricate machines that register earthquakes ten thousand miles away" — Gatsby's responsiveness likened to a seismograph.
- "they stood on my shelf in red and gold **like** new money from the mint" — the books like fresh currency.
- "two young women were buoyed up as though upon an anchored balloon" — the couch/women like a tethered balloon.
- "their dresses were rippling and fluttering **as if** they had just been blown back in after a short flight" — the women as if alighting from flight.
- "blew curtains in at one end and out the other **like** pale flags" — curtains as flags.
- "making a shadow on it **as** wind does on the sea" — rug-shadow like wind on water.
- "each light deserting her with lingering regret **like** children leaving a pleasant street at dusk" — fading sunlight like reluctant children.
- "compelled me from the room **as though** he were moving a checker to another square" — Tom moving Nick like a game piece.
- "sitting down at the table **as if** she were getting into bed" — Jordan's languor.
- "as cool as their white dresses" — the women's banter like their dresses.
- "as if to a vigil beside a perfectly tangible body" — Tom and Jordan retreating to the library as if to a deathwatch.
- "opening up again in a flower-like way" — Daisy blossoming.
