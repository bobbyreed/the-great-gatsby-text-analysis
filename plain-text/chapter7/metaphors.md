# Metaphors — Chapter 7

A scholarly catalogue of the metaphors in Chapter 7 of *The Great Gatsby* — the
novel's longest and most violent chapter (the Plaza confrontation and Myrtle's
death). Each entry quotes the figurative language and notes what is being
compared (tenor → vehicle). Genuine metaphors are listed first; notable similes
follow.

## Metaphors

- **"his career as Trimalchio was over"** — Gatsby cast as Trimalchio, the vulgar host of Petronius's *Satyricon*.
- **"the whole caravansary had fallen in like a card house at the disapproval in her eyes"** — Gatsby's household/social world as a collapsing house of cards.
- **"The straw seats of the car hovered on the edge of combustion"** — the heat figured as near-ignition.
- **"In this heat every extra gesture was an affront to the common store of life."** — vitality as a shared, depletable reserve.
- **"Daisy and Jordan lay upon an enormous couch, like silver idols, weighing down their own white dresses against the singing breeze of the fans"** — the women as silver idols; the fan-breeze as singing.
- **"a tiny gust of powder rose from her bosom"** — powder as a gust.
- **"Her voice struggled on through the heat, beating against it, moulding its senselessness into forms."** — Daisy's voice as a body fighting and shaping the heat.
- **"Her voice is full of money ... the jingle of it, the cymbals' song of it"** — Daisy's voice as money/coin music. (The chapter's central metaphor.) Followed by **"High in a white palace the king's daughter, the golden girl"** — Daisy as a fairy-tale golden princess.
- **"the dead dream fought on ... struggling unhappily, undespairingly, toward that lost voice across the room"** — Gatsby's dream personified as a dying combatant.
- **"trying to touch what was no longer tangible"** — the past as something that has lost solidity.
- **"the compressed heat exploded into sound"** — heat figured as a bursting pressure-vessel (the wedding march).
- **"he saw himself standing alone on the last barrier of civilization"** — Tom's racial panic as a lone defender of a fortress.
- **"Flushed with his impassioned gibberish"** — Tom's rhetoric named gibberish.
- **"the portentous menacing road of a new decade"** — Nick's thirties as a threatening road ahead.
- **"the promise of a decade of loneliness, a thinning list of single men ... a thinning brief-case of enthusiasm, thinning hair"** — aging rendered as a series of thinning inventories.
- **"So we drove on toward death through the cooling twilight."** — the literal drive made an emblematic journey toward death.
- **"Tom was feeling the hot whips of panic"** — panic as a lash.
- **"His wife and his mistress, until an hour ago secure and inviolate, were slipping precipitately from his control."** — Tom's possessions slipping from his grasp.
- **"one emotion after another crept into her face like objects into a slowly developing picture"** — Myrtle's expressions as images emerging in a photographic developing bath.
- **"the giant eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg kept their vigil"** — the billboard eyes personified again as a watchman (recurring symbol).
- **"only the dead dream fought on"** (see above) — repeated emphasis on the dream as a dying body.
- **"the words seemed to bite physically into Gatsby"** — words as biting teeth.
- **"gave up the tremendous vitality she had stored so long"** — Myrtle's life as hoarded vital energy released at death.
- **"They were gone ... snapped out, made accidental, isolated, like ghosts even from our pity."** — the departing pair as ghosts.
- **"the foreign clamor on the sidewalk or the tumult of the elevated"** — Tom's voice rendered as remote noise.

## Notable similes and related figurative language

- "like silver idols" — Daisy and Jordan.
- "my underwear kept climbing **like** a damp snake around my legs" — bodily discomfort in the heat.
- "as remote ... **as** the foreign clamor on the sidewalk" — Tom's voice.
- "**like** objects into a slowly developing picture" — Myrtle's shifting emotions.
- "her left breast was swinging loose **like** a flap" — the brutal image of Myrtle's death.
- "Tom tapped his thick fingers together **like** a clergyman" — false piety.
- "Picking up Wilson **like** a doll" — Wilson's helplessness.
- "his eyes ... pale as his own pale hair" — Wilson's sickness.
- "the abounding blessed isles" — the seascape idealized.
